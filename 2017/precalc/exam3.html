The two line segments that join the foci of an ellipse to a point on an ellipse are called focal radii. The distance from the first focus to the point is represented by the equation $$ d_1 = a + ex_0$$ The distance from the second focus to the point can be represented in a similar fashion. $$ d_2 = a - ex_0 $$ Ellipses can be defined as the set of points P whose distances to the focus and the directrix are in a fixed ration between 0 and 1. The equation of the directrices can be found with the following equation: $$ x \text{ (or y)} = (-) {a\over e} $$ The equation of the tangent line to the ellipse ${x^2\over a^2} + {y^2\over b^2} = 1$ at the point $P(x_1,y_1)$ is equal to $${x_1x\over a^2} + {y_1y\over b^2} = 1$$ Similar to a parabola, an ellipse has reflectve properties. In this case, if a ray leaves one of the foci and collides with a point of on the ellipse, it will reflect off and pass through the other focus.

Hyperbolas

Definition: The set of all points in the plane, the absolute value of the differences of whose distances from two fixed points is a positive constant. Each fixed point is called a focus.

Transverse Axis- the line segment that joins the two vertices.
Conjugate Axis- the line segment that is perpendicular to the transverse axis and extends a distance b on either side of the center of the hyperbola.

Also note, $$ |PF_1-PF_2| = 2a $$ The absolute value is something that should be especially noted. The equation for a hyperbola with a horizontal focal axis is $$ {x^2\over a^2} - {y^2\over b^2} = 1$$ The equation for a hyperbola with a vertical focal axis is $${y^2\over a^2} - {x^2\over b^2} = 1$$ As for values for vertices, foci, etc. the values go in the x position generally for horizontal and vice versa. The equation of the asymptotes for horizontal is $y = \pm {b \over a}x$ and vice versa for vertical. The same rules apply for off-centered hyperbolas. The only thing to note regarding these is that the equations of the asymptotes are in point-slope form such that they form an equation like $y-k=\pm{b\over a}(x-h)$. In order to find the equation of the hyperbola given the asymptotes is to generally graph it and find the quadrant.
The latera rectum of a hyperbola has the length ${2b^2 \over a}$ and the endpoints change opposite to how the regular value shifting works. E.g. $(h \pm c, k \pm {b^2 \over a}) $ and vice versa.
Focal radii for a hyperbola are the same as for an ellipse, with the exception being that you always take the absolute value of the expression.
The equation of the tangent is also the same with the exception being that it is minus, that is, it is ${x_1x\over a^2} - {y_1y\over b^2} = 1$
Finally, hyperbolas also have reflective properties. A ray that starts from one focus, will reflect off the hyperbola in a direction that goes directly away from the other focus. A ray directed to one focus, will reflect in the direction of the other focus (outside).
